Gestational Diabetes

Carbohydrate intolerance first recognized during pregnancy. Understand risk factors, screening, management, and maternal/fetal complications to optimize outcomes.

Introduction & Risk Factors

Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM) is glucose intolerance that begins or is first diagnosed during pregnancy. It affects ~7–14% of pregnancies globally and increases maternal and fetal risks.
👩 Risk Factors (High Risk)
• Marked obesity (BMI ≥30)
• Previous GDM or macrosomic infant (>4000g)
• Family history of diabetes (first-degree relative)
• Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
• Maternal age >35 years
• Certain ethnic groups (Hispanic, African American, Native American, Asian)
🔬 Moderate Risk Factors
• Age 25–35, overweight (BMI 25–29.9),
• Previous poor obstetric history (stillbirth, congenital anomalies)
• Hypertensive disorders in pregnancy

Pathophysiology & Screening

  • Pathophysiology: Pregnancy induces insulin resistance via human placental lactogen (hPL), progesterone, cortisol, and TNF‑α. In GDM, pancreatic β‑cells cannot compensate → hyperglycemia.
  • Screening timing: Universal screening at 24–28 weeks. High‑risk women may be screened earlier (first trimester) and repeated if normal.
  • Two‑step approach (ACOG): 50g glucose challenge test (GCT) → 1‑hour plasma glucose ≥130–140 mg/dL → 100g oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) for diagnosis.
  • One‑step approach (IADPSG/WHO): 75g OGTT at 24–28 weeks, fasting, 1h, 2h levels. Used by many institutions.
⚠️ Women at very high risk (previous GDM, obesity, strong family history) should be screened at first prenatal visit.

Diagnosis & Classification

🧪 Two‑step Diagnostic Criteria (Carpenter‑Coustan)
100g OGTT – plasma glucose (mg/dL):
Fasting ≥95, 1h ≥180, 2h ≥155, 3h ≥140. Two or more elevated → GDM.
📊 One‑step (IADPSG/WHO)
75g OGTT – fasting ≥92, 1h ≥180, 2h ≥153. One or more elevated → GDM.
  • Class A1: Diet‑controlled GDM (normal fasting glucose).
  • Class A2: GDM requiring medication (insulin or oral hypoglycemic).

Management & Treatment

  • Medical Nutrition Therapy (MNT): Calorie prescription based on BMI (1800–2400 kcal/day). Carbohydrate distribution (3 meals + 2–3 snacks). Limit simple sugars. Small, frequent meals.
  • Self‑monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG): Fasting and 1‑ or 2‑hour postprandial levels. Targets: fasting <95 mg/dL, 1h <140 mg/dL, 2h <120 mg/dL.
  • Exercise: Walking, swimming, stationary bike (30 min most days).
  • Insulin therapy: First‑line pharmacologic agent when MNT fails. Lispro or aspart preferred for prandial, NPH for basal. Insulin does not cross placenta.
  • Oral hypoglycemics: Metformin and glyburide may be used but not FDA‑approved specifically for GDM; some studies raise concerns about neonatal hypoglycemia.

Maternal & Fetal Complications

👩 Maternal
• Preeclampsia
• Polyhydramnios
• Preterm labor
• Cesarean delivery (due to macrosomia)
• Postpartum hemorrhage
• Future type 2 diabetes (50–70% within 10 years)
👶 Fetal/Neonatal
• Macrosomia (>4000–4500g) → shoulder dystocia, birth trauma
• Neonatal hypoglycemia (due to fetal hyperinsulinemia)
• Respiratory distress syndrome (delayed surfactant)
• Polycythemia, hyperbilirubinemia
• Stillbirth (rare with good glycemic control)
• Childhood obesity and type 2 diabetes later in life

Nursing Role & Patient Education

  • Education: Teach glucose monitoring technique, target ranges, meal planning, sick day management, and signs of hypoglycemia (sweating, tremor, dizziness).
  • Medication teaching: Insulin injection technique, storage, rotation sites. Hypoglycemia treatment (15g carb rule).
  • Fetal surveillance: Non‑stress tests (NST), biophysical profile (BPP) starting at 32–34 weeks if on medication or poor control.
  • Intrapartum management: Maintain glucose 70–110 mg/dL with insulin drip if needed. Monitor fetal heart rate closely.
  • Postpartum care: Check fasting glucose before discharge. Glucose tolerance test (75g OGTT) at 6–12 weeks to rule out persistent diabetes. Encourage breastfeeding (reduces future diabetes risk for mother and child).
